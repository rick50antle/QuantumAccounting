# MEMORANDUM

**To:** John Fellingham, Haijin Lin, Doug Schroeder
**From:** Rick Antle
**Date:** April 4, 2026
**Re:** "An Information Property of Double Entry Accounting" — notes on structure versus magnitudes

---

I've been thinking about a new way to introduce the paper that focuses attention on the accounting structure — specifically, on what is common across very different institutional settings that all use double entry. Here are two proposed opening paragraphs, followed by the reasoning behind them.

> Double-entry accounting systems share a common architectural feature: they maintain multiple articulating financial statements whose entries must simultaneously satisfy more than one identity. In a for-profit entity, the income statement explains *why* equity changed — distinguishing earnings from capital contributions — while the cash flow statement reconciles accrual and cash measures of the same events. In a nonprofit, the operating statement distinguishes changes in restricted from unrestricted net assets, conveying whether donor restrictions have been satisfied. In governmental fund accounting, each fund maintains its own balance sheet and operating statement that must independently articulate, carrying structural information through separate reconciling channels even though the right side of each fund's identity is simply "fund balance." In every case, individual account balances convey magnitude information — how much cash, how large a receivable. But the requirement that the same events be recorded on multiple statements in structurally different yet reconciled ways adds information-carrying capacity that no single ledger of balances could provide.

> This paper develops an algebra for analyzing the use of articulated statements to convey information. We model the double-entry system as a communication channel in the sense of Hartley (1928) and Shannon (1948). Journal entries are encoded as two-by-two matrices, T-accounts are represented as two-element vectors, and posting is matrix-vector multiplication. Within this framework, all non-cash transactions reduce to combinations of two operations: reclassification and revaluation. We show that one account can transmit two bits of information — distinguishing among four types of events — but only when the system maintains at least two accounts on multiple articulating financial statements that simultaneously satisfy the accounting identity and the cash flow reconciliation identity. The capacity result does not depend on magnitudes, on any particular decomposition of equities, or on whether the entity is for-profit, nonprofit, or governmental. It depends on the architecture: multiple statements, recording the same events in structurally different ways, constrained to reconcile.

## Supporting reasoning

**Magnitudes and the asset/liability distinction.** Our paper removes scale entirely, reducing all accounts to a balance of one. This isolates the structural contribution; the two-bit result is a floor on capacity, not a ceiling. But we can push further. The asset/liability distinction is itself a magnitude phenomenon — a "negative asset" is a liability. Interest rate swaps illustrate this: the same contract sits on either side of the balance sheet depending on the sign of its fair value. This is consistent with Definition 2(ii), which treats accounts on the same statement as indistinguishable. Every within-statement distinction is a magnitude distinction.

**The irreducible structural distinction.** What survives when magnitudes are removed? The accounting identity, Assets = Equities, separates resources from claims — these are not positive and negative versions of the same quantity. But this identity alone is nearly trivial. The capacity comes from requiring the same events to be recorded on multiple articulating statements in structurally different but reconciled ways. Proposition 2 establishes this: two-bit capacity requires each account to appear on both statements in structurally different roles. Only 4 of 16 configurations achieve this. The Theorem generalizes: one account can transmit two bits whenever at least two accounts satisfy both the accounting identity and the cash flow reconciliation identity.

**The equity boundary.** The structural capacity is engaged only when equities are affected. Asset swaps and liability settlements change magnitudes but don't use the multi-statement architecture — a single-entry system would convey the same information. When equities change, the system must distinguish *why* — earnings versus contributions, restricted versus unrestricted releases, operating versus financing sources. That is when multiple statements articulate to convey information no single ledger could.

## Next steps

I think this reframing strengthens the paper in three ways. First, we make the positive case for abstracting magnitudes: the structure carries independent information about the *sources* of changes in wealth, not just the changes themselves. Second, the equity-boundary observation identifies precisely *when* the structural capacity is engaged. Third, the generality across for-profit, nonprofit, and fund accounting strengthens the case that the result is about architecture, not institutional convention. I would welcome your thoughts on whether and how to incorporate these ideas.
