# Continuation Doc: "An Information Property of Double Entry Accounting"

## Context

Rick Antle is working with coauthors John Fellingham, Haijin Lin, and Doug Schroeder on the paper "An Information Property of Double Entry Accounting" (March 2025). The paper models the double-entry accounting system as a communication channel using information theory (Shannon 1948) and quantum information (Pauli matrices). T-accounts are two-element vectors, journal entries are 2x2 matrices (X = reclassification, Z = revaluation), and the central result is that one account can transmit two bits of information when two articulating financial statements and at least two accounts satisfy the accounting identity and cash flow reconciliation identity.

## What we discussed

We had an extended conversation about the modeling foundations, specifically the vector representation of accounts and the decision to abstract away from magnitudes. The discussion built through several stages:

### 1. Abstracting away magnitudes is reasonable
The paper's goal is to study the information-carrying capacity of the *structure* of double entry, not the data flowing through it. Removing magnitudes isolates the structural contribution. The two-bit result is a floor on capacity, not a ceiling.

### 2. Asset vs liability is a magnitude distinction
A "negative asset" is a liability. Interest rate swaps show this: the same contract sits on either side of the balance sheet depending on the sign of fair value. The A vs L label is just the sign bit of a continuous variable. This is consistent with Definition 2(ii) in the paper. Every within-statement distinction is a magnitude distinction.

### 3. The irreducible structural distinction is resources vs claims
The accounting identity A = E separates resources from claims. These are not positive and negative versions of the same quantity. A receivable does not become equity by changing sign. This is the genuinely structural boundary.

### 4. A = E alone is nearly trivial — articulation is what matters
One identity with magnitudes removed carries almost no information. The capacity comes from requiring the same events to be recorded on multiple articulating statements (accrual + cash flow) in structurally different but reconciled ways. Two simultaneous constraints create exploitable redundancy.

### 5. The equity boundary is the dividing line
Only when equities are affected does the extra structural capacity get used. Asset swaps (trade equipment for investment), liability settlements (pay off AP with cash) — these convey magnitude information but don't engage the structural capacity. A single-entry system tracking balances could convey the same thing. The X operator crosses the equity boundary (capitalizing vs expensing). The Z operator generates gains/losses that flow through equity. The two-bit capacity is about distinguishing four different answers to "how was equity affected?"

### 6. Equities decompose into fundamentally different sources
Contributed by owners vs generated by transactions/events — this is a difference in kind, not magnitude. The income statement exists to make this distinction: recording *why* wealth changed, not just *that* it changed.

### 7. The result is general, not context-specific
The paper's result is NOT about the for-profit decomposition of equities (earned vs contributed). It is about the architecture adding capacity whenever multiple articulating representations must reconcile. Same mechanism works for:
- **For-profit**: earned vs contributed equity
- **Nonprofit**: restricted vs unrestricted net assets; information about satisfaction of donor restrictions
- **Government fund accounting**: right side is just "fund balance" — no earned/contributed decomposition. System compensates by proliferating funds, each self-balancing with its own articulating statements.

The capacity is a property of the architecture, not the institutional context.

## Rick's conclusions

- The equity boundary is the correct dividing line for when structural capacity is engaged
- The result's generality across for-profit, nonprofit, and fund accounting is an important strength
- The memo proposes three improvements to the paper's framing: (1) make the positive case for abstracting magnitudes rather than just asserting tractability; (2) identify the equity boundary as when structural capacity is engaged; (3) emphasize generality across institutional settings
